An analysis of Bureau of Labor Statistics Current Population Survey microdata pooled across January through April 2026 reveals that the official unemployment rate of 4.36% understates the full extent of labor underutilization in the United States. When involuntary part-time workers and marginally attached individuals — those who want work and have looked for it, but not in the last four weeks — are counted, the figure rises to 7.44%, a broad underutilization rate nearly double the headline. Beyond those who lack sufficient hours, 15.01% of employed Americans hold credentials that measurably exceed the typical educational attainment of their occupation, suggesting a structural misalignment between workforce capacity and available roles. Together, these measures paint a more complete portrait of the labor market than any single statistic can: one in which the headline announces stability while broader indicators point to a quieter, more pervasive form of economic underperformance.
