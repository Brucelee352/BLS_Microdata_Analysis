An analysis of Bureau of Labor Statistics Current Population Survey microdata pooled across January through May 2026 finds an official unemployment rate of 4.36% — a figure independently validated against the BLS's own published not-seasonally-adjusted Table A-15 data, where the arithmetic mean of monthly NSA values is identical at 4.36%. When involuntary part-time workers and marginally attached individuals are included, the broad underutilization rate rises to 7.44% by this project's microdata calculation, compared to 8.10% in the BLS published NSA average; the 0.66-percentage-point gap reflects a narrower marginal-attachment filter applied here (PEDWWNTO, PEDWAVL, and PEDWLKWK survey responses) rather than any error in the U-3 methodology. Separately, 15.01% of employed Americans are classified as overqualified using the realized-matches method: for each occupation with at least 30 observations, the mean and standard deviation of educational attainment are computed, and any worker whose education exceeds the occupation mean by more than one standard deviation is counted — a relative, data-driven threshold that requires no external credential crosswalk. Together, these measures describe a labor market in which the headline understates underutilization, our U-3 holds up exactly against official benchmarks, and one in six employed workers carries more education than their role statistically demands.
