The numbers, taken at face value, speak of a labor market at something like equilibrium. 4.36% — that is the official unemployment rate for the first four months of 2026, drawn directly from Bureau of Labor Statistics Current Population Survey microdata and weighted to represent the full working-age population of the United States. It is a number precise enough to feel trustworthy. But precision is not the same as completeness, and that is where the story begins to widen.

Add in those who have looked for work in the past year but not the past four weeks — the marginally attached, the quietly discouraged — and those who punch a part-time clock not by choice but by necessity, and the rate becomes 7.44%. That is the U-6, the Bureau's broadest measure of labor underutilization, and it is nearly double the figure most headlines will ever print. Then look further still: 15.01% of employed Americans are working jobs their education outpaces by a statistically meaningful margin. One in six. Present, accounted for, clocked in — and running at well below their capacity. Overqualification, in this analysis, is defined not by any external judgment but by the data itself: workers whose educational attainment exceeds the typical level for their occupation by more than one standard deviation, measured across nearly half a million survey records.

There is no single cause the data can name — and to claim one would be to outrun the evidence. What the microdata can say, clearly and without overreach, is this: the gap between what the official rate announces and what the broader measures reveal is not a footnote. It is a structural feature of how we count, and how we fail to count, the full complexity of work. If labor market analysis is going to serve the people it describes, it has to begin from measures honest enough to hold their whole experience. This dashboard is a step in that direction — state by state, measure by measure, worker by worker.
