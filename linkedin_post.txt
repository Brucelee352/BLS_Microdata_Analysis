The numbers, taken at face value, speak of a labor market at something like equilibrium. 4.36% — that is the official unemployment rate for the first five months of 2026, drawn from Bureau of Labor Statistics Current Population Survey microdata and weighted to represent the full working-age population of the United States. It is a number precise enough to feel trustworthy. And in this case, the precision holds up: when cross-validated against the BLS's own published not-seasonally-adjusted Table A-15 figures for January through May 2026, the microdata estimate matches the official monthly NSA average exactly, to the hundredth of a percentage point. The methodology is sound. The story, however, is not finished there.

Extend the count to workers who have looked for employment in the past year but not the past four weeks, and to those clocking part-time hours not by preference but by necessity, and the rate rises to 7.44% by this project's calculation — compared to 8.10% in the BLS's own published broad measure. That 0.66-percentage-point gap is not a discrepancy to hide; it is a disclosed methodology boundary. The marginal-attachment filter applied here — drawing on three specific CPS survey responses about job desire, availability, and recent search — captures a narrower population than the Bureau's full accounting. The gap falls in that margin, not in the unemployment count itself. Then there is overqualification: 15.01% of employed Americans hold credentials that exceed the statistical norm for their current occupation, as measured by the realized-matches method. For each occupation with at least 30 survey respondents, the mean and standard deviation of educational attainment are computed; anyone whose education clears that occupation's mean by more than one standard deviation is counted. No external credential database, no O*NET crosswalk, no imposed threshold — only what the data itself reveals about who is working where, and whether their education fits the room.

There is no single cause the data can name — and to claim one would be to outrun the evidence. What the microdata can say, clearly and without overreach, is this: the gap between what the official rate announces and what the broader measures reveal is not a footnote. It is a structural feature of how we count, and how we fail to count, the full complexity of work. A validated U-3. A disclosed U-6 gap. One in six workers overqualified by their occupation's own internal standard. If labor market analysis is going to serve the people it describes, it has to begin from measures honest enough to hold their whole experience — and honest enough to name, clearly, where the measure ends and the limit begins.
